Email 2: The $16/Month Story

Send: Day 3 after signup

Mautic email name: bac-welcome-02-stack-story

Subject: My entire marketing stack costs $16/month

Alt subject: How I replaced $800/month of SaaS with open-source tools

Preview text: The real version. Including everything that went wrong.

Body:

Hey {contactfield=firstname|there},

When I started Nunya Bunya, I had a problem that every new business owner has: I needed email marketing, analytics, a CRM, a booking system, link tracking, and social scheduling. The "standard" answer is HubSpot + Calendly + Mailchimp + Google Analytics + Bitly. That's about $300-800/month before you've made a dollar.

I couldn't afford that. So I didn't pay it.

Instead, I built the entire stack on open-source tools, self-hosted on a single Hetzner server that costs me about $16/month AUD.

Here's what runs on it:

  • Mautic -- email marketing and automation (replaces Mailchimp/HubSpot)
  • Matomo -- web analytics (replaces Google Analytics)
  • Twenty CRM -- contact and deal management (replaces HubSpot CRM)
  • n8n -- workflow automation (replaces Zapier)
  • Shlink -- link tracking (replaces Bitly)
  • Cal.com -- appointment booking (replaces Calendly)

That's the clean version. Here's what actually happened.

The first time I set up Mautic, I misconfigured the mail transport and it bounced every email for three days before I noticed. My Matomo install broke twice during updates. I spent an entire weekend debugging an n8n workflow that turned out to have a single misplaced bracket. The CRM migration ate a Saturday I'll never get back.

But once it worked, it worked. And now I run email campaigns, track every website visitor, manage leads, automate outreach, and book calls -- across multiple businesses -- for the price of two coffees a month.

I'm not saying everyone should do this. If you have the budget for paid tools, they're easier to set up. But if you're bootstrapping and you're willing to learn, the open-source path is real and it works.

The Solopreneur Stack one-pager has the full list with links to every tool. If you want to go deeper on any of them, just reply and tell me which one -- I'll point you to the best setup guides I've found.

-- Ben

Email 3: Behind the Scenes -- What I'm Working on Right Now

Send: Day 7 after signup

Mautic email name: bac-welcome-03-bts

Subject: What I'm actually working on this week

Alt subject: Behind the scenes -- April 2026 update

Preview text: The honest version of running too many businesses at once.

Body:

Hey {contactfield=firstname|there},

This email is the format you'll get from me going forward -- an honest look at what I'm working on, what's going well, and what's a mess.

What I'm building right now:

Nunya Bunya (the agency) is the main business. We help small businesses and solopreneurs set up their marketing from scratch -- websites, email, social, the whole system. Most of our clients are local Brisbane businesses who've been burned by agencies that charge a lot and deliver PowerPoint decks. We actually build the thing.

Power Portraits is a portrait photography studio. Headshots, personal branding shoots, the kind of photos that make people look like the version of themselves they see in their head. I started it because I kept getting asked to shoot headshots and realized there was a business in it.

Pawesome Dog Walkers is exactly what it sounds like. I walk dogs in Brisbane's inner north. It started as a cash flow play while the other businesses grew, and honestly, it's become one of the most enjoyable parts of my week. Dogs don't care about your conversion rates.

ORCA Film Awards is a passion project -- an independent awards body that nominates overlooked and underseen films. Think of it as the anti-Oscars. We recruit volunteer critics and voters who actually watch the films the Academy ignores.

What's going well:

The marketing systems are humming. Email automations are running, content is going out consistently, and the self-hosted stack is stable (finally). Pawesome is getting bookings through word of mouth, which is the best kind of marketing.

What's hard:

Time. There is never enough of it. ADHD makes context-switching between businesses feel like changing gears in a car with a broken clutch. Some days I build an entire email funnel before lunch. Other days I stare at a blank screen for forty-five minutes and then walk the dog instead.

This is what building looks like. It's not pretty, but it's real.

More soon,

Ben

Email 4: The YouTube Pilot

Send: Day 14 after signup

Mautic email name: bac-welcome-04-youtube

Subject: I'm starting a YouTube channel. Here's why.

Alt subject: Episode 1 is coming. Let me tell you about it.

Preview text: A docuseries about building businesses from scratch. No tutorials. No vlogs.

Body:

Hey {contactfield=firstname|there},

I've been sitting on this for a while, so here it is: I'm making a YouTube channel.

Not a tutorial channel. Not a vlog. A docuseries.

The concept is simple. Each episode pairs a film review with a real chapter from my life building these businesses. The film provides the lens, my life provides the story. Think essay film meets founder diary.

Episode 1 is called "No Other Choice." It's paired with the Park Chan-wook film of the same name -- a story about a master craftsman who gets cut loose from the system and has to rebuild on his own terms. The parallel to what I'm doing felt too obvious to ignore.

The episode covers the first phase of building Nunya Bunya: registering the business, buying the domain, building the website, setting up the marketing machine, printing flyers, knocking on doors. All of it filmed in a fast-cut montage that's designed to feel like competence, not content.

I'm not interested in making videos where I sit in front of a camera and explain things. I want to make something that feels like a film. Shallow depth of field. Sound design. Colour grading. The kind of thing where someone watches it and thinks "this person actually knows how to make things."

If that sounds like something you'd watch, I'd genuinely appreciate a YouTube subscribe. It helps more than you'd think, especially early on.

Subscribe on YouTube

I'll let you know when Episode 1 drops.

-- Ben

Email 5: What Would You Build?

Send: Day 30 after signup

Mautic email name: bac-welcome-05-engagement

Subject: One question for you

Alt subject: I'm curious about something

Preview text: What are you building? I want to know.

Body:

Hey {contactfield=firstname|there},

You've been on this list for about a month now, so I want to ask you something directly.

What are you building?

It doesn't have to be a business. It could be a creative project, a career shift, a side hustle, a skill you're developing. Whatever it is that you're putting energy into right now -- I want to hear about it.

I ask because the most useful conversations I've had in the last year haven't been with mentors or business coaches. They've been with people who are in the middle of building something and willing to be honest about where they're stuck.

So if you've got thirty seconds, hit reply and tell me:

  1. What you're working on
  2. The one thing that's giving you the most trouble right now

I'll read every reply. If I can help, I will. If I can't, I'll tell you that too.

This isn't a sales pitch. I'm not going to funnel you into a course or a coaching program. I'm asking because I'm genuinely interested, and because the best ideas I've had for my own businesses have come from conversations exactly like this one.

Talk soon,

Ben
