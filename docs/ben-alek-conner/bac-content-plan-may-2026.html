Ben Alek Conner -- May 2026 Content Plan

Platforms: Twitter (primary), LinkedIn (professional), Instagram (visual)

Cadence: 3x per week (Mon / Wed / Fri)

Voice: First person, personal, honest, slightly self-deprecating. No corporate tone. No motivational poster energy.

Rules: No fabricated stats. No fake testimonials. No numeric guarantees. No emojis.

Week 1: May 4-8

Post 1 -- Monday, May 4

Type: Building in public

Platforms: Twitter, LinkedIn

Twitter:

I've been running my marketing agency for over a year and my entire tech stack costs $16/month. Self-hosted Mautic, Matomo, n8n, Twenty CRM, Cal.com, Shlink. One Hetzner server. That's it. The setup took a painful weekend. The savings have been worth every hour of debugging.

LinkedIn:

When I started Nunya Bunya, I couldn't afford HubSpot, Mailchimp, Calendly, and the rest. So I built the whole stack on open-source tools, self-hosted on a single server for about $16/month AUD.

Mautic for email. Matomo for analytics. n8n for automation. Twenty for CRM. Cal.com for booking. Shlink for link tracking.

Did it break multiple times during setup? Yes. Did I spend a weekend debugging a single misplaced bracket in an automation workflow? Also yes.

But it works now. And it runs email campaigns, website analytics, lead management, and appointment booking for multiple businesses -- for the cost of two coffees a month.

Not everyone should do this. But if you're bootstrapping and willing to learn, the open-source path is real.

Post 3 -- Friday, May 8

Type: Business lesson

Platforms: Twitter, LinkedIn

Twitter:

Biggest lesson from running multiple businesses simultaneously: you don't balance them. You prioritise ruthlessly and accept that at least one of them is being neglected at any given time. The skill is knowing which one can survive a week of inattention.

LinkedIn:

The question I get asked most often is "how do you run multiple businesses at once?"

The honest answer: badly, most of the time, in at least one of them.

You don't balance multiple businesses. You triage. Every week I decide which business gets my full attention and which ones run on the systems I've already built. Some weeks the agency needs me. Some weeks Pawesome gets the energy. Some weeks the photography studio is the priority.

The trick is building each business so it can survive a week without you. Automations, templates, SOPs, scheduled content. Not because I'm lazy -- because I'm one person and there are only so many hours.

It's not glamorous. But it works.

Post 6 -- Friday, May 15

Type: Business lesson

Platforms: Twitter, LinkedIn

Twitter:

Unpopular opinion: most small businesses don't need a social media manager. They need a system that lets them post consistently without hiring someone. A content calendar, a batch shooting day, a scheduling tool. Three things. That's it.

LinkedIn:

I run a marketing agency and I'm going to say something that sounds like it's against my own interests:

Most small businesses don't need to hire a social media manager.

What they need is a system. A content calendar with a month of ideas. One afternoon of batch shooting. A free scheduling tool. That's three things, and it takes one weekend to set up.

The problem isn't that business owners can't create content. It's that nobody taught them a system for doing it efficiently. So they either post randomly when they remember, or they pay someone $2,000/month to post mediocre content on their behalf.

There's a better way. Build the system first. Hire the person later, when you actually know what good looks like.

Post 9 -- Friday, May 22

Type: Business lesson

Platforms: Twitter, LinkedIn

Twitter:

Three things I'd tell myself on day one of starting an agency:

  1. Your first client will come from someone you already know. Stop cold emailing strangers and start telling friends what you do.
  2. Build the systems before you need them. You won't have time later.
  3. The website doesn't need to be perfect. It needs to exist.

LinkedIn:

If I could go back to the day I registered Nunya Bunya, here's what I'd tell myself:

Your first client will come from your existing network. Not from a cold email, not from an ad, not from a viral post. From someone who already trusts you. Tell everyone you know what you're building. That's your marketing strategy for month one.

Build your systems before you have clients. Email templates, onboarding docs, a CRM, a booking page. You will not have time to build these once work starts coming in. Do it now while you have the luxury of an empty calendar.

Ship the website. It doesn't need to be perfect. It needs to exist. A mediocre website that's live beats a beautiful website that's still in development. You can improve it every week for the rest of your life.

None of this is revolutionary. But I didn't do any of it, and it cost me time I didn't have.

Production Notes

  • Batch write all 15 posts in one sitting. Scheduling takes 30 minutes after that.
  • Photography posts require actual new photos. Shoot 1-2 sessions in late April to have May content ready.
  • YouTube teaser posts (10, 15) should only go live once filming is confirmed. Adjust dates if Episode 1 timeline shifts.
  • All LinkedIn posts should be plain text. No links in the body (kills reach). Put links in the first comment.
  • Twitter posts should be standalone thoughts. No threads unless the idea genuinely requires it.
  • Instagram captions can be longer but the photo has to carry the post. If the photo isn't strong, don't post it.
