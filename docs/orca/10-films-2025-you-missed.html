10 Films from 2025 You Probably Missed

10 Films from 2025 You Probably Missed

By ORCA Editorial | April 19, 2026

Every year, hundreds of extraordinary films slip through the cracks. They premiere at festivals, earn glowing reviews, secure limited theatrical runs in a handful of cities, and then vanish — replaced in the cultural conversation by whatever franchise sequel is dominating the multiplex. The films on this list are the ones that deserved better. They are the reason the ORCAs exist.

These are all real films that released in 2025. You may not have heard of most of them. You should fix that.

1. All We Imagine as Light

Director: Payal Kapadia | Country: India/France | Language: Hindi/Marathi

Payal Kapadia's debut narrative feature won the Grand Prix at Cannes in 2024 and then rolled out theatrically through 2025, becoming one of the most acclaimed films of the year. Set in Mumbai, it follows two Malayali nurses — one older and trapped in a marriage to an absent husband, one younger and navigating a forbidden relationship — as they journey from the relentless city to the quiet coast of Ratnagiri.

Kapadia, who previously made the documentary A Night of Knowing Nothing, brings a documentarian's eye for texture and a poet's sense of rhythm to every frame. The film moves like a dream dissolving into memory. It is luminous, patient, and devastating.

All We Imagine as Light was India's first film in Cannes Competition in 30 years. It won nothing at the Oscars. It was nominated for Best Director and Best International Feature but went home empty-handed. The film that India waited three decades to send to the world's biggest film festival was acknowledged and then set aside.

Where to watch: Available on Janus Films/MUBI in select territories.

2. The Brutalist

Director: Brady Corbet | Country: USA/UK/Hungary | Language: English/Hungarian

Brady Corbet's three-and-a-half-hour epic about a Hungarian Jewish architect who survives the Holocaust and emigrates to America was one of the most ambitious films of the decade. Adrien Brody delivers a career-redefining performance as László Tóth, a man whose artistic vision collides with the brutal realities of American capitalism and patronage.

Shot on VistaVision — the first narrative feature to use the format in decades — The Brutalist is a film about what America does to the people who come to it with dreams. It is punishing, beautiful, and completely uncompromising. Corbet won Best Director at Venice for it.

While The Brutalist did receive Oscar nominations (including Best Picture), Corbet's uncompromising vision represents exactly the kind of filmmaking ORCA champions: work that refuses to be comfortable, that demands patience, and that rewards it.

Where to watch: A24 theatrical and home video release.

3. Grand Tour

Director: Miguel Gomes | Country: Portugal/Italy/France | Language: Portuguese/English

Miguel Gomes won Best Director at Cannes 2024 for this sprawling, genre-defying film that follows a British diplomat fleeing his fiancee across Asia in 1917 — though the film is less interested in the chase than in the landscapes, faces, and rhythms of the places he passes through. Part period drama, part travelogue, part dream journal, Grand Tour shifts between black-and-white 16mm fiction and color documentary footage shot in contemporary Myanmar, Thailand, Vietnam, Japan, and China.

Gomes is one of the most inventive filmmakers working in cinema today. His Tabu (2012) and the three-part Arabian Nights (2015) established him as a director willing to blow up narrative conventions and rebuild them from scratch. Grand Tour continues that tradition. It is a film that asks you to surrender to its logic rather than impose your own.

It received no Oscar nominations. It was barely released in the United States.

Where to watch: Limited theatrical via Grasshopper Film; available in European territories.

4. Caught by the Tides

Director: Jia Zhangke | Country: China | Language: Mandarin

Jia Zhangke has spent 25 years chronicling China's transformation with an intimacy and scope that no other filmmaker matches. Caught by the Tides, which premiered at Cannes 2024 and screened throughout 2025, is both a fiction film and a time capsule — assembling footage shot over two decades (some of it previously unused material from Jia's earlier films) into a story about a woman searching for a man who abandoned her, set against the backdrop of China's economic explosion.

Zhao Tao, Jia's wife and longtime collaborator, is extraordinary as the lead — aging across 20 years of real footage, her performance spanning not just a character's life but the life of a nation. The film is a meditation on memory, loss, and the way countries reinvent themselves while people stay the same.

Jia Zhangke has never been nominated for an Oscar. One of the most important filmmakers of the 21st century, and the Academy does not know he exists.

Where to watch: Limited theatrical; available on MUBI in select regions.

5. No Other Land

Directors: Basel Adra, Hamdan Ballal, Yuval Abraham, Rachel Szor | Country: Palestine/Norway | Language: Arabic/Hebrew/English

No Other Land won Best Documentary at the Berlin Film Festival in 2024 and went on to become one of the most discussed and debated documentaries of 2025. Shot over five years in the West Bank community of Masafer Yatta, the film documents the forced displacement of Palestinian families by Israeli military demolitions through the eyes of Basel Adra, a Palestinian activist, and Yuval Abraham, an Israeli journalist.

The film is urgent, unflinching, and deeply personal. It does not pretend to neutrality — it is a film made by people whose homes are being destroyed — but its power comes from its specificity and its patience. It shows you what displacement looks like day by day, demolition by demolition, document by document.

No Other Land struggled to find US distribution for months despite its Berlin prize. It was eventually nominated for Best Documentary Feature at the Oscars and won, but its path to that stage was far harder than it should have been for a film of this caliber.

Where to watch: Theatrical release via Cinephil; streaming availability varies by region.

6. Nickel Boys

Director: RaMell Ross | Country: USA | Language: English

RaMell Ross adapted Colson Whitehead's Pulitzer Prize-winning novel about two Black teenagers at a brutal Florida reform school in the Jim Crow era — and he did it almost entirely in first-person POV. The camera becomes the eyes of the characters. You do not watch their experience; you inhabit it.

It is one of the most formally radical American films in years, and it works not because of its technique but because Ross makes the technique serve the story's emotional truth. The subjective camera is not a gimmick — it is the only honest way to tell a story about people whose perspective has been systematically erased.

Nickel Boys received Oscar nominations but was largely overlooked in the major categories relative to its ambition and achievement. It is the kind of film that will be studied and discussed for decades, regardless of what the Academy thought of it.

Where to watch: Amazon MGM Studios theatrical and streaming.

7. Dahomey

Director: Mati Diop | Country: France/Senegal/Benin | Language: French/Fon

Mati Diop's documentary about the repatriation of 26 royal treasures from France's Musee du Quai Branly to Benin won the Golden Bear at Berlin 2024 — the festival's highest prize — and screened widely through 2025. At just 68 minutes, it is dense with ideas about colonialism, restitution, and the meaning of cultural heritage.

Diop frames the artifacts not as objects but as witnesses. A voiceover spoken by one of the treasures gives the film an eerie, almost supernatural quality — these are not just historical artifacts being shipped home; they are beings returning from exile. The second half shifts to a student debate in Cotonou about whether the repatriation is meaningful or merely symbolic, and the film refuses to resolve that tension.

Dahomey received no Oscar nominations. A Golden Bear winner — the top prize at one of the three most important film festivals in the world — completely invisible to the Academy.

Where to watch: MUBI.

8. Hard Truths

Director: Mike Leigh | Country: UK | Language: English

Mike Leigh's latest is a portrait of Pansy, a middle-aged Black British woman consumed by rage, anxiety, and unprocessed grief. Marianne Jean-Baptiste — who was Oscar-nominated 28 years ago for Leigh's Secrets & Lies — delivers one of the most fearless performances of the decade. Pansy is abrasive, cruel, exhausting, and utterly human. Jean-Baptiste makes you understand every layer of pain beneath the anger.

Leigh's method — extensive improvisation and rehearsal to build characters from the ground up — produces a specificity that scripted films rarely achieve. Hard Truths is not a comfortable watch. It is a necessary one.

Jean-Baptiste was nominated for Best Actress at the Oscars but did not win. The film itself received no other nominations. Mike Leigh, one of the greatest living directors, remains largely invisible to the Academy outside the acting categories.

Where to watch: Bleecker Street theatrical release; home video in 2025.

9. Vermiglio

Director: Maura Delpero | Country: Italy/France/Belgium | Language: Italian/Trentino dialect

Maura Delpero's second feature won the Grand Jury Prize (Silver Lion) at Venice 2024 and became Italy's submission for Best International Feature at the Oscars. Set in a remote Alpine village during the final year of World War II, it follows a large family whose lives are disrupted when a Sicilian deserter arrives and falls in love with one of the daughters.

Vermiglio is a film of extraordinary visual beauty — the mountain landscapes are photographed with the reverence of a painter and the precision of a geographer — but its real power lies in Delpero's ability to render an entire community with equal attention to each member. It is a choral film in the truest sense, and its portrait of how war distorts even the most isolated lives is quietly devastating.

It was nominated for Best International Feature at the Oscars but received no recognition beyond that single category.

Where to watch: Sideshow/Janus Films theatrical; streaming availability forthcoming.

10. The Seed of the Sacred Fig

Director: Mohammad Rasoulof | Country: Germany/France/Iran | Language: Persian

Mohammad Rasoulof made this film in secret in Iran and then fled the country to premiere it at Cannes 2024, where it won the Special Jury Prize. Set during the 2022 Mahsa Amini protests, it follows an investigating judge whose promotion forces him deeper into the regime's machinery of repression while his wife and two daughters are radicalized by the protest movement happening outside their apartment.

The Seed of the Sacred Fig is a thriller built on real political terror. Rasoulof incorporated actual protest footage — shot by demonstrators at enormous personal risk — into the film's narrative. The result is a work of fiction that carries the weight of documentary. Every scene vibrates with the knowledge that the filmmaker risked imprisonment or worse to make it.

Rasoulof was sentenced to prison and flogging by the Iranian government. He smuggled himself out of Iran to attend the Cannes premiere. The film was nominated for Best International Feature at the Oscars but received no other recognition from the Academy. A filmmaker who risked his freedom to make a film about political courage, and the best the Academy could offer was a single nomination in a category most voters barely pay attention to.

Where to watch: Neon theatrical release; streaming on MUBI in select territories.

The Pattern

Look at this list. An Indian film that won the Grand Prix at Cannes. A Chinese film from one of the century's most important directors. A Senegalese-French documentary that won the Golden Bear at Berlin. A film made in secret by a director who fled his country to show it to the world. These are not obscure curiosities. They are some of the most significant works of cinema produced in 2025, and the Academy either ignored them entirely or acknowledged them with the bare minimum.

This is why the ORCAs exist. Not to compete with the Oscars, but to ensure that the films they overlook are not forgotten. Every film on this list is worth your time. Every film on this list matters. Start watching.
