ORCA Film Awards 2026 — Judge Scoring Rubric

Confidential — For Judges Only

2026 Awards Season

Foreword from the Founder

You are reading this because you have agreed to serve as a judge for ORCA Film Awards. That is not a small thing.

Independent filmmakers have spent months — in many cases, years — bringing their work to life. When they submit to ORCA, they are trusting us with something irreplaceable: their vision, their effort, their story. Your role is to evaluate that work honestly, rigorously, and with the care it deserves. Not to find films that match your personal taste — but to assess with clarity what each film achieves on its own terms.

The scores you give will determine who is nominated. The deliberations you participate in will determine who wins. That responsibility is real. We ask you to honour it.

Thank you for your time, your expertise, and your commitment to independent cinema.

— Ben Alek Conner, Founder & Director, ORCA Film Awards

Guiding Principle: Score each category independently. A film may score 9 in Cinematography and 4 in Screenplay — this granularity is valuable. Do not allow your impression of one category to colour your score in another. Budget and production scale are not scoring criteria. A film made for $500 can and should outperform a film made for $500,000 if the craft merits it.

Section 2: Category Scoring Criteria

CATEGORY 1: DIRECTION

Out of 10 — Evaluating the filmmaker's creative vision, leadership, and command of the medium.

Direction is the central creative intelligence of the film. The director shapes every element — the performances drawn from actors, the frames chosen, the pace at which the story unfolds. A strong directorial hand is visible without being intrusive. The film feels authored.

9–10 — Exceptional

The direction demonstrates a singular and fully realised creative vision. Every creative choice — shot composition, performance direction, tonal consistency, pacing — serves the film's purpose with precision. The director's voice is unmistakable. This film could only have been made this way, by this person.

7–8 — Excellent

Strong directorial vision is consistently evident throughout the film. The director demonstrates clear intent and skilled execution. There may be minor inconsistencies, but these do not undermine the overall achievement. The film has a coherent and intentional identity.

4–6 — Competent

The direction shows genuine competence and craft. A point of view is present, though it may not be fully realised or sustained throughout. Some sequences demonstrate real skill; others feel less assured. The film holds together, but the directorial voice occasionally gives way to convention.

1–3 — Below Standard

Directorial intent is unclear, inconsistent, or ineffective. The film lacks a coherent creative point of view. Choices appear arbitrary or confused. The filmmaking does not serve the story or demonstrates a fundamental lack of command over the medium.

Consider: Is there a consistent directorial vision? How effectively does the director work with performers? Are creative decisions deliberate or accidental?

CATEGORY 2: SCREENPLAY / STORY

Out of 10 — Evaluating narrative construction, dialogue, character, and thematic depth.

Note: For documentaries, evaluate narrative structure, subject selection, and thematic coherence rather than written screenplay.

9–10 — Exceptional

The screenplay demonstrates mastery of story construction. Narrative architecture is clear and purposefully executed. Characters are fully realised with authentic motivation and emotional depth. Dialogue is distinctive and purposeful — it reveals character, advances story, or illuminates theme. The film has something meaningful to say and says it with craft.

7–8 — Excellent

The screenplay is strong, with well-constructed narrative and compelling characters. The story moves with purpose. Dialogue is competent and often effective. Themes are present and functional. There may be minor structural issues, but the screenplay forms a solid foundation for the film.

4–6 — Competent

The screenplay shows real craft in parts. The story is coherent and the central dramatic situation is engaging. Characters have defined arcs, though motivation may occasionally feel thin. Dialogue is functional but may fall into convention. Themes exist but may be stated rather than dramatised.

1–3 — Below Standard

The screenplay does not support the film. Narrative is unclear or lacks coherent structure. Characters are thin. Dialogue is weak or purely expository. There is little discernible thematic intent.

Consider: Does the story have a compelling dramatic purpose? Are characters three-dimensional and motivated? Does the film have something meaningful to say?

CATEGORY 3: CINEMATOGRAPHY

Out of 10 — Evaluating visual storytelling, composition, lighting, and camera craft.

9–10 — Exceptional

The cinematography is a major contributor to the film's power. Every frame is composed with intention. Lighting serves mood, character, and theme. Camera movement is deliberate — when the camera moves, it means something. The visual language of the film is distinctive and entirely appropriate to the story.

7–8 — Excellent

Strong, purposeful cinematography that effectively serves the story. Compositions are consistently well-considered. Lighting creates the required mood. Camera choices feel deliberate. There may be occasional technical inconsistencies, but the overall visual execution is commendable.

4–6 — Competent

Solid cinematography that is functional and, in places, genuinely effective. Images serve the story without distracting from it. Some compositions are striking; others are conventional. Technical execution is largely sound. The cinematography does not distinguish itself but does not undermine the film.

1–3 — Below Standard

Significant technical or creative deficiencies undermine the visual experience. Compositions are consistently poor, lighting is ineffective, or technical problems significantly detract from the work.

Note on low-budget films: Evaluate what the cinematographer achieves with available resources. A resourceful, visually coherent low-budget aesthetic can score higher than an expensive but visually empty production.

CATEGORY 4: EDITING AND PACING

Out of 10 — Evaluating rhythm, structure, continuity, and the invisible art of assembly.

9–10 — Exceptional

The editing demonstrates a mastery of rhythm and tempo. Cuts are made at precisely the right moment. The film breathes — it has space when it needs space and momentum when the story demands it. The editing is largely invisible: you feel the story, not the assembly. In sequences of complexity, the editing demonstrates exceptional control.

7–8 — Excellent

Strong, purposeful editing throughout. The film moves well. Scene transitions are effective. The emotional rhythm is well-managed. There may be occasional cuts that could be refined, but overall editorial work serves the film with skill.

4–6 — Competent

Editing is functional and largely effective. The story flows coherently. Pacing is generally appropriate. There may be scenes that run slightly long or cuts that don't land as intended, but these are not severely disruptive.

1–3 — Below Standard

Significant pacing or structural issues caused by the editing. The film feels poorly assembled. Scenes do not work in sequence, rhythmic choices undermine tension or emotion, or the editing draws constant unwanted attention to itself.

CATEGORY 5: SOUND DESIGN AND SCORE

Out of 10 — Evaluating the film's sonic world: dialogue, effects, ambience, and music.

9–10 — Exceptional

The sound design creates a complete and immersive world. Dialogue is clear and appropriately textured. Sound effects and ambience serve the narrative with intelligence — silence is used as effectively as sound. Music (where present) is integrated with intention and precision. The sonic landscape is a distinct creative achievement.

7–8 — Excellent

Strong sound design that serves the film effectively. Dialogue is clear. Ambience and effects create a believable world. Music is well-integrated. Minor issues may exist, but overall sound work is commendable.

4–6 — Competent

Functional sound design that does not significantly distract. Dialogue is largely intelligible. Ambience is present. The sound serves the story adequately if not distinctively.

1–3 — Below Standard

Significant audio issues that meaningfully undermine the film. Unintelligible dialogue, persistent technical problems, or jarring audio choices that damage the film's credibility.

CATEGORY 6: PRODUCTION DESIGN

Out of 10 — Evaluating art direction, set design, costuming, props, and location choices.

Note: For low-budget and no-budget films, evaluate how effectively available resources are used to create a coherent visual world. Resourcefulness is not penalised — carelessness is.

9–10 — Exceptional

Production design is a fully realised and distinctive element of the film. Every location, costume, and prop choice contributes meaningfully to the world of the story. The visual world is coherent, specific, and serves both narrative and theme. Design choices feel authored, not accidental.

7–8 — Excellent

Strong, purposeful production design that effectively supports the story. Locations are well-chosen and dressed appropriately. Costuming serves character. The visual world is consistent and believable.

4–6 — Competent

Production design is adequate and does not distract. Visual choices are reasonable. There may be inconsistencies or missed opportunities, but the world of the film is broadly established.

1–3 — Below Standard

Production design is absent, inconsistent, or actively harmful to the film's credibility. Visual elements contradict the story world or feel accidental and unconsidered.

CATEGORY 7: PERFORMANCES

Out of 10 — Evaluating authenticity, range, and emotional truth in all on-screen presence.

Note: For documentaries with interview subjects, evaluate how effectively the filmmaker draws out authentic, compelling human presence. For animation, evaluate voice performances.

9–10 — Exceptional

The performances in this film are genuinely outstanding. Lead performance(s) demonstrate a level of truth and presence that feels fully inhabited. Characters feel like real people in real circumstances. A performance, a scene, a look — something here will stay with you.

7–8 — Excellent

Strong performances throughout. Leads are compelling and believable. The emotional register feels authentic. There may be minor moments where a performance reaches for something and falls slightly short, but these are exceptions.

4–6 — Competent

Performances are solid and largely effective. Leads handle the material competently. Emotional moments land adequately. There may be uneven moments or supporting performances of varying quality, but the central performances support the film.

1–3 — Below Standard

Performances significantly detract from the film. Lack of authenticity or technical control prevents meaningful emotional engagement with the story.

CATEGORY 8: EMOTIONAL IMPACT

Out of 10 — Evaluating the film's total emotional and intellectual effect on the viewer.

9–10 — Exceptional

This film matters. It leaves a mark. Whether through emotion, provocation, beauty, humour, or truth — it achieves an impact that places it beyond mere competent filmmaking. You will be thinking about this film after the credits roll. The sum of its parts exceeds any individual element. It is the kind of film that reminds you why independent cinema exists.

7–8 — Excellent

A genuinely affecting and accomplished film. Its impact is real and lingering. Strong in its emotional or intellectual delivery. A film that works on its own terms and fully delivers on its promise.

4–6 — Competent

A satisfying film that achieves its intended effect. Impact is present and appropriate. May not be transformative, but it is well-crafted independent work that deserves to be seen.

1–3 — Below Standard

The film does not achieve meaningful impact. The viewing experience may feel flat, incoherent, or emotionally disengaged.

CATEGORY 9: ORIGINALITY AND INNOVATION

Out of 10 — Evaluating the distinctiveness of voice, approach, and creative contribution.

9–10 — Exceptional

The film presents a genuinely distinctive creative voice or makes a meaningful formal, narrative, or conceptual contribution to cinema. It does not feel derivative. It could not easily be confused with another film. The filmmaker is saying something in a way that feels entirely their own.

7–8 — Excellent

The film demonstrates a clear individual perspective and shows genuine creative ambition. While it may work within familiar forms, it does so with a freshness and specificity that lifts it above the generic.

4–6 — Competent

The film has a recognisable creative sensibility. It may work within well-established conventions but does so with craft. Moments of genuine originality are present even if the whole is not entirely distinctive.

1–3 — Below Standard

The film is highly derivative, follows genre convention without purpose or personality, or demonstrates no meaningful individual creative voice.

CATEGORY 10: TECHNICAL EXECUTION

Out of 10 — Evaluating overall professional execution across all technical departments.

9–10 — Exceptional

The film demonstrates outstanding technical execution across all visible departments. Technical craft serves the creative vision with complete fluency. There is nothing in the technical presentation that distracts from or undermines the work. The film is technically complete and polished to a standard appropriate for professional exhibition.

7–8 — Excellent

Strong technical execution throughout. The film is well-made at a production level. Minor technical issues may exist but do not affect the viewing experience meaningfully.

4–6 — Competent

Technical execution meets an adequate standard. The film is watchable and the technical presentation does not significantly distract. Some elements may be rough, but they do not undermine the work.

1–3 — Below Standard

Significant technical deficiencies across multiple departments. The film's technical presentation undermines the creative work. Issues in this category would prevent professional exhibition.

Section 4: Judging Instructions

Before You Begin Judging

  1. Read this rubric in full. You are responsible for understanding the criteria before assessing any film.
  2. Confirm your assigned films and categories with the Head of Judging.
  3. Complete and submit your Conflict of Interest Declaration before accessing any screeners.
  4. Access the judge portal at judges.orcafilmawards.com.au using your provided credentials.

Conflict of Interest Declaration

You must declare and recuse yourself from scoring any film in which you have:

  • A personal relationship (friend, family, close colleague) with the director, producer, or any key cast or crew member
  • A financial interest or commercial relationship with the production or its producers
  • A prior professional collaboration (within the past 3 years) with a key creative on the film
  • Any other circumstance that a reasonable person would consider a conflict of interest

When in doubt, declare. It is always better to recuse yourself than to score a film under a conflict. Contact the Head of Judging immediately upon identifying a potential conflict.

Scoring Independence

  • Score each film independently, without discussion with other judges, until the formal deliberation phase.
  • Do not seek out or share scores with other judges before deliberation.
  • Base all scores on the criteria in this rubric — not personal taste, prior reputation, perceived budget, or production pedigree.

Written Comments

Each category score must be accompanied by written comments (minimum 2 sentences). These comments are:

  • Used during the deliberation process by all judges
  • In some cases, shared in curated form as feedback to filmmakers
  • A critical part of the integrity and transparency of ORCA's process

Write comments you would be proud to defend publicly. Be honest, be specific, and be fair.

Section 5: Confidentiality

All judging materials, screener links, scores, and deliberation discussions are strictly confidential.

  • Do not discuss the content of any film you are judging with anyone outside the formal judging process
  • Do not post about specific films, scoring outcomes, or deliberations on social media — during or after the judging period
  • Do not disclose scores, shortlists, or nominees to any third party before the official public announcement
  • All screener links are for your use only — do not share them under any circumstances
  • This confidentiality obligation is ongoing and does not expire after the awards ceremony

Breach of confidentiality may result in your removal from the judging panel and exclusion from future ORCA judging.

Section 6: Score Submission Process

  1. Access the judge portal at judges.orcafilmawards.com.au using your provided credentials
  2. For each assigned film, complete the scoring form — one score and comment per category
  3. Review your total score before submitting
  4. Submit; you will receive a confirmation email
  5. Scores may be revised until the deliberation window opens (date communicated by Head of Judging)
  6. All scores must be submitted by the date specified by the Head of Judging — late scores cannot be accepted once deliberation has begun

Queries: Contact the Head of Judging directly. Do not contact ORCA's public communications channels with judging matters.
