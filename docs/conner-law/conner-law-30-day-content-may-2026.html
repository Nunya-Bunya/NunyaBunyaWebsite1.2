WEEK 2: UNDERSTANDING INJURIES

May 8-14 | TBI, CRPS, workplace injuries -- why understanding your injury matters for your case

DAY 4 -- May 8 (Thursday)

PLATFORM: Facebook

TYPE: Educational / Know Your Rights

CAPTION:

"Traumatic brain injuries do not always look dramatic.

Most people picture a TBI as someone unconscious on a stretcher. The reality is that many traumatic brain injuries happen without any loss of consciousness at all. A hard jolt to the head in a car accident can cause a concussion or diffuse axonal injury that does not show up on a standard CT scan.

Symptoms that might mean more than 'just a headache' after an accident:

  • Persistent headaches that get worse, not better
  • Difficulty concentrating or remembering things
  • Sensitivity to light or noise
  • Mood changes, irritability, or anxiety that was not there before
  • Trouble sleeping or sleeping far more than usual

Insurance companies routinely undervalue brain injuries because they are hard to see on imaging. That does not mean they are not real. That does not mean they are not life-altering.

If you hit your head in an accident -- or even if your head was jolted hard without direct impact -- tell your doctor. Document everything. And do not accept a settlement that only covers your ER visit when you are dealing with symptoms that may last months or years.

This is general information, not legal advice. Every case is different.

CAPTION:

"CRPS: one of the most painful conditions in medicine, and one of the hardest to prove in court.

Complex Regional Pain Syndrome can develop after an injury -- sometimes a relatively minor one. The pain is disproportionate to the original injury, spreads beyond the initial site, and can become permanent if untreated.

The challenge in CRPS litigation is that insurance companies love to argue it is 'subjective.' No broken bone on an X-ray. No tumor on an MRI. Just a patient reporting severe, burning pain that has taken over their life.

Winning a CRPS case requires:

  • Early and thorough medical documentation from specialists, not just your GP
  • Objective diagnostic tests (bone scans, thermography, nerve conduction studies)
  • Expert witnesses who can explain the condition to a jury in plain language
  • An attorney who has actually handled CRPS cases before and knows the medical landscape

Not every PI firm takes CRPS cases. Many firms refer them out because they require more work. We take them because we have seen what happens when nobody fights for these clients.

This is general information, not legal advice. Every case is different.

CAPTION:

"If you were hurt at work because of someone else's negligence, you may have more options than workers' comp alone.

Workers' compensation covers your medical bills and a portion of lost wages. But it does not cover pain and suffering, and the payouts are capped.

Here is what many injured workers do not know: if a third party caused your workplace injury -- a subcontractor, equipment manufacturer, property owner, or negligent driver -- you may have a separate personal injury claim on top of workers' comp.

Examples:

  • A delivery driver hits you while you are working on a job site
  • Defective equipment injures you (manufacturer liability)
  • A property owner's negligence creates a hazardous work environment
  • A subcontractor's crew causes an accident on a shared construction site

Workers' comp and a third-party personal injury claim are not mutually exclusive. You can pursue both.

This is general information, not legal advice. Your situation may be different.

TYPE: Process / How PI Law Works

CAPTION:

"What 'no win, no fee' actually means -- because most people assume there is a catch.

There is no catch.

Here is how contingency fee arrangements work in personal injury law:

  1. You pay nothing upfront. Zero retainer. Zero hourly billing.
  2. We investigate your case, deal with the insurance company, hire experts, build your claim -- all at our expense.
  3. If we recover money for you, our fee comes out of the settlement or verdict. You know the percentage before you sign anything.
  4. If we do not recover money for you, you owe us nothing. We absorb the loss.

Why do PI attorneys work this way? Because it aligns our interests with yours. We do not get paid unless you get paid. That means we only take cases we believe in, and we fight harder because our income depends on your outcome.

The insurance company has a team of lawyers on salary. No win, no fee means you get a lawyer who is equally motivated to maximize your result.

This is general information, not legal advice.

TYPE: Community / Safety Awareness

CAPTION:

"Washington State's wet roads are not just a winter problem.

Spring rain creates a deceptive driving hazard: oil and road grime that accumulated during dry spells rises to the surface when rain begins, creating an extremely slick layer. The first 10-15 minutes of rain after a dry spell are often the most dangerous.

Washington drivers deal with rain constantly, which creates a false sense of confidence. Familiarity with wet roads does not make you immune to hydroplaning, reduced visibility, or increased stopping distances.

For employers with field staff, delivery drivers, or anyone whose job puts them on Washington roads:

  • Ensure fleet vehicles have adequate tire tread
  • Build extra time into schedules during wet conditions
  • Remind drivers that hydroplaning starts at approximately 35 mph on standing water

For everyone else: slow down, increase following distance, and turn your headlights on. Washington law requires headlights when wipers are on (RCW 46.37.020).

Road safety is everyone's responsibility.

"Distracted driving kills more than 3,000 people in the United States every year (NHTSA data).

Washington State banned handheld phone use while driving in 2017 (E-DUI law, RCW 46.61.672). But the law alone has not solved the problem. We still see distracted driving as a factor in accident after accident.

What counts as distracted driving:

  • Texting or scrolling (the obvious one)
  • Eating or drinking
  • Adjusting GPS or music
  • Talking to passengers while navigating complex traffic
  • Reaching for items in the car

If someone was texting, scrolling, or otherwise distracted when they hit you, that is evidence of negligence. It strengthens your personal injury claim significantly. Preserving phone records and dashcam footage early in the case can make a major difference.

Your safety matters. And if someone else's distraction caused your injury, your right to fair compensation matters too.

LEGAL COMPLIANCE NOTES

  1. Disclaimers are mandatory. Every post that discusses legal outcomes, rights, statutes, or process must include a disclaimer that it is general information, not legal advice, and that every case is different. This is not optional.
  2. No fabricated results. Never post specific settlement amounts, win rates, or case outcomes unless they are verified, real, and the client has given written consent to share. Use no numbers that cannot be independently verified.
  3. No guarantees. Never say "we will win your case" or promise specific outcomes. Use language like "you may have a claim," "your case could be worth more," and "contact us to discuss your specific situation."
  4. No fake testimonials. If client testimonials are used in future content, they must be real, with the client's written consent. Never fabricate quotes, names, or scenarios.
  5. Statute references. When citing Washington statutes (RCW), verify the citation is current. Statutes referenced in this plan: RCW 4.16.080 (3-year statute of limitations for PI), RCW 46.37.020 (headlights with wipers), RCW 46.61.672 (distracted driving / E-DUI).
  6. Spanish-language outreach. Include the Spanish-language phone line on all Facebook posts. Se habla espanol is a service differentiator and accessibility feature.
