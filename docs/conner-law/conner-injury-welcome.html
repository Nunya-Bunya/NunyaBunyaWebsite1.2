Email 2: When the Insurance Company Calls

Subject: What to say (and NOT say) when the insurance company calls

Preview Text: One wrong sentence could cost you thousands. Here's how to protect yourself.

Body:

<p>Dear {{contact.firstname|default('Friend')}},</p>

<p>If you've been in an accident, there's a good chance the other driver's insurance company will call you — sometimes within hours. They'll sound friendly. They'll say they just want to "get your side of the story."</p>

<p><strong>Be very careful.</strong></p>

<p>Here's what the insurance adjuster won't tell you: everything you say in that call is being recorded and can be used to reduce or deny your claim.</p>

<p>One of our clients — we'll call him James — was T-boned at an intersection by a driver who ran a red light. The at-fault driver's insurance company called James the next day. Feeling cooperative, James told them he was "doing okay" and that his back was "a little sore but not too bad."</p>

<p>Weeks later, when his back pain turned out to be a serious disc injury requiring surgery, the insurance company pointed to his own words to argue his injuries weren't that severe.</p>

<p><strong>James came to Conner Injury Law.</strong> We fought back, brought in medical experts, and secured a settlement that fully reflected his injuries and recovery. But it would have been much simpler if he'd spoken with us before that first call.</p>

<p><strong>Three rules if the insurance company contacts you:</strong></p>

<ol>
  <li>Be polite, but don't give a recorded statement</li>
  <li>Don't discuss the details of the accident or your injuries</li>
  <li>Tell them your attorney will be in touch — even if you haven't hired one yet</li>
</ol>

<p>If you have any questions about dealing with insurance companies, we're always happy to help — no charge, no obligation.</p>

<p>Sincerely,<br>
<strong>Conner Injury Law</strong></p>
