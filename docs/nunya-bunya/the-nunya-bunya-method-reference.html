THE 20 BOOKS

Tier 1: The Original 8

1. Guerrilla Marketing — Jay Conrad Levinson (1984)

Big Idea: Time, energy, and imagination beat money. Every time.

Key Frameworks:

  • 200 Marketing Weapons (most are free)
  • Fusion Marketing (partner with complementary businesses)
  • The Marketing Calendar (no calendar = no discipline = no results)
  • Consent Marketing (earn attention before selling)

Key Principles:

  • Follow-up is everything — 68% of lost business is poor follow-up, not bad products
  • Say one thing well per piece of marketing
  • Repetition breeds familiarity, familiarity breeds trust
  • Your best new customers are your current customers
  • Profits, not revenue — measure what matters

Best Quote: "Marketing is not an event, but a process. It has a beginning, a middle, but never an end."

2. $100M Offers — Alex Hormozi (2021)

Big Idea: Make offers so good people feel stupid saying no.

Key Frameworks:

  • The Value Equation: Value = (Dream Outcome x Perceived Likelihood) / (Time Delay x Effort & Sacrifice)
  • Grand Slam Offer: Stack so much value that price becomes irrelevant
  • Offer Creation Process: (1) Identify dream outcome (2) List every obstacle (3) List solutions for each (4) Bundle into offer
  • The 4 Enhancers: Scarcity + Urgency + Bonuses + Guarantees

Key Principles:

  • Charge premium — cheap prices attract bad clients
  • Price on value, not cost
  • Commoditized = compared. Differentiated = desired
  • Starving crowds beat clever marketing (pick the right market)

Best Quote: "The goal is not to decrease price — it's to increase value."

3. Building a StoryBrand — Donald Miller (2017)

Big Idea: The customer is the hero. You're the guide.

Key Frameworks:

  • SB7 Framework: (1) Character wants something (2) Has a problem (external, internal, philosophical) (3) Meets a guide (4) Who gives them a plan (5) Calls them to action (6) Helps them avoid failure (7) Ends in success
  • The BrandScript: One-page DNA for all marketing
  • The One-Liner: Problem + Solution + Result
  • The Grunt Test: Can someone look at your site and in 5 seconds know what you offer, how it helps, and what to do?

Key Principles:

  • If you confuse, you lose
  • The internal problem is what actually sells (not the external one)
  • A 3-step plan reduces anxiety and increases conversion
  • People don't buy the best products — they buy the ones they understand fastest

Best Quote: "Story is the greatest weapon we have to combat noise."

4. Traction — Gabriel Weinberg & Justin Mares (2014)

Big Idea: Spend 50% of your time on product, 50% on distribution.

Key Frameworks:

  • The Bullseye Framework: Brainstorm all 19 channels → Test top 3 → Focus on the winner
  • 19 Traction Channels: Viral, PR, Unconventional PR, SEM, Social Ads, Offline Ads, SEO, Content, Email, Engineering as Marketing, Blogs, BD, Sales, Affiliates, Existing Platforms, Trade Shows, Events, Speaking, Community
  • The 50% Rule: Half your time = distribution. Always.
  • The Critical Path: Define the traction goal that changes everything, work backward

Key Principles:

  • Most businesses fail from lack of distribution, not bad products
  • Every channel eventually saturates — restart Bullseye when it does
  • Cheap tests first — hundreds of dollars, not thousands
  • Product-channel fit matters — your product shapes which channels work

Best Quote: "Almost every failed startup has a product. What failed startups don't have is enough customers."

5. This Is Marketing — Seth Godin (2018)

Big Idea: Find the smallest viable market and obsess over them.

Key Frameworks:

  • "People like us do things like this" — Culture drives buying, not features
  • Status Roles — Every transaction involves status (raising it or protecting it)
  • The Lock and Key — Find the lock (unmet need), then craft the key
  • Direct vs Brand Marketing — Know which one you're doing. Don't apply direct metrics to brand work.

Key Principles:

  • Reach the right people, not the most people
  • Price is a signal — cheap is a race to the bottom
  • Tension moves people from interested to enrolled
  • Marketing is a generous act of helping someone solve a problem

Best Quote: "It's not for everyone. Saying this is a feature, not a limitation."

6. Purple Cow — Seth Godin (2003)

Big Idea: Remarkable or invisible. There is no middle.

Key Frameworks:

  • The TV-Industrial Complex — The old buy-ads-get-distribution cycle is dead
  • The New P — Purple Cow added to Product, Price, Place, Promotion
  • Sneezers — Build for obsessed early adopters who spread the word
  • Go to the edges — Find the extreme of some attribute rather than the comfortable middle

Key Principles:

  • Remarkability is baked into the product, not added by marketing after the fact
  • "Very good" is the same as invisible
  • Safe is risky — playing it safe guarantees mediocrity
  • Target the otaku (obsessives), not the mainstream

Best Quote: "In a crowded marketplace, fitting in is failing."

7. Influence: The Psychology of Persuasion — Robert Cialdini (1984)

Big Idea: 7 psychological triggers drive nearly all human decisions.

Key Frameworks — The 7 Principles:

  1. Reciprocity — Give first, people feel obligated to return
  2. Commitment & Consistency — Small yes leads to bigger yes
  3. Social Proof — People follow what others do
  4. Authority — People defer to credible experts
  5. Liking — People buy from people they like
  6. Scarcity — Limited availability increases perceived value
  7. Unity — Shared identity creates tribal belonging

Key Principles:

  • These work as automatic shortcuts — people react, not deliberate
  • Ethical use = highlighting what genuinely exists. Fabrication = manipulation that destroys trust
  • Rejection-Then-Retreat: ask big first, then ask for what you actually want

Best Quote: "The way to love anything is to realize that it may be lost."

8. Ogilvy on Advertising — David Ogilvy (1983)

Big Idea: Advertising exists to sell. If it doesn't sell, it isn't creative.

Key Frameworks:

  • The Big Idea Test: (1) Did it make you gasp? (2) Do you wish you'd thought of it? (3) Is it unique? (4) Can it run for 30 years?
  • The 80% Rule: 5x more people read the headline than the body. The headline IS the ad.
  • Research-First: Test everything. Study what actually works (response data), not what people say they like.

Key Principles:

  • Headlines that promise a benefit outperform all others
  • Specifics beat generalities ("At 60mph..." vs "luxury ride")
  • Long copy outsells short copy — people who read past the headline are real prospects
  • Photos outsell illustrations. Captions are read 2x more than body copy.
  • Write conversationally, as if speaking to one person

Best Quote: "The consumer is not a moron, she is your wife."

Tier 2: The Additional 12

9. Positioning — Al Ries & Jack Trout (1981)

Big Idea: Marketing is a battle of perceptions, not products. Own a word in the prospect's mind.

Key Framework: If you can't be first in a category, create a new category you can be first in.

Why It Matters: A small business will never out-feature a corporation. But it can own a position in a niche. "Personal injury in WA" or "headshot photographer in Brisbane" — that's ownable territory.

10. Blue Ocean Strategy — W. Chan Kim & Renee Mauborgne (2005)

Big Idea: Stop competing in crowded markets. Create uncontested space.

Key Framework: The Four Actions Framework — Eliminate, Reduce, Raise, Create. Redesign your value proposition so comparison is impossible.

Why It Matters: Small businesses can't win a price war. But they can redefine the playing field entirely.

11. Permission Marketing — Seth Godin (1999)

Big Idea: Earn the privilege of delivering messages to people who want them.

Key Framework: Turn strangers into friends, friends into customers, by trading value for attention at every step.

Why It Matters: Interruption marketing requires massive budgets. Permission marketing requires trust — which small businesses build faster than corporations.

12. Breakthrough Advertising — Eugene Schwartz (1966)

Big Idea: Match your message to your prospect's awareness level.

Key Framework: 5 Stages of Customer Awareness — Unaware → Problem Aware → Solution Aware → Product Aware → Most Aware. Each stage needs a different message.

Why It Matters: Small businesses waste money showing "buy now" to people who don't know they have a problem. Segment by awareness, craft the right message for each stage. Costs nothing extra.

13. Crossing the Chasm — Geoffrey Moore (1991)

Big Idea: Most products die between early adopters and the mainstream.

Key Framework: Dominate one narrow beachhead segment completely before expanding. Early adopters buy on vision; the majority buys on proof.

Why It Matters: Don't try to be everything to everyone. Own one niche, become the reference case, then expand.

14. Contagious — Jonah Berger (2013)

Big Idea: Virality isn't luck — it's engineered.

Key Framework: STEPPS — Social Currency, Triggers, Emotion, Public, Practical Value, Stories.

Why It Matters: Small businesses can't buy reach. But they can engineer shareability using this checklist.

15. Made to Stick — Chip Heath & Dan Heath (2007)

Big Idea: Some ideas survive and others die. Here's why.

Key Framework: SUCCESs — Simple, Unexpected, Concrete, Credible, Emotional, Stories.

Why It Matters: Corporations repeat mediocre messages a million times. Small businesses get one shot to be remembered. This is the formula.

16. How Brands Grow — Byron Sharp (2010)

Big Idea: Brands grow by acquiring new buyers, not increasing loyalty.

Key Framework: Mental Availability (being thought of) + Physical Availability (being easy to buy). Distinctive brand assets matter more than differentiated positioning.

Why It Matters: Stop obsessing over loyal customers. Focus on being visible, memorable, and easy to buy from. Reach beats frequency.

17. The 22 Immutable Laws of Marketing — Al Ries & Jack Trout (1993)

Big Idea: 22 laws that govern marketing success like gravity governs physics.

Key Laws: Law of Leadership (be first), Law of Category (create a new one), Law of Focus (own one word), Law of the Opposite (if you're #2, play opposite of #1).

Why It Matters: These laws are MORE powerful for small businesses. "Create a new category" is the ultimate small business weapon.

18. The Long Tail — Chris Anderson (2006)

Big Idea: The internet made niche markets collectively larger than hit markets.

Key Framework: Democratized production + democratized distribution + filter power = viable niche businesses.

Why It Matters: You don't need mass appeal. You need to be findable by the specific people who want exactly what you offer. The long tail validates every niche business.

19. They Ask, You Answer — Marcus Sheridan (2017)

Big Idea: Answer every question your customers are asking — especially the uncomfortable ones.

Key Framework: The Big 5 content topics driving 80% of sales traffic: Pricing, Problems, Comparisons, Reviews, "Best Of" lists.

Why It Matters: Zero ad budget required. One blog post answering "how much does X cost?" generated $3M+ in revenue. Meet prospects at the moment of need with honest answers.

20. Duct Tape Marketing — John Jantsch (2007)

Big Idea: Marketing is a system, not random tactics.

Key Framework: The Marketing Hourglass — Know → Like → Trust → Try → Buy → Repeat → Refer.

Why It Matters: Written specifically for small businesses with no marketing team. The complete system for owners doing it themselves.

THE 12 INDELIBLE PRINCIPLES

The truths that were true in 1960, are true in 2026, and will be true in 2050. These don't change with technology.

  1. People buy with emotion, justify with logic — (Cialdini, Ogilvy, neuroscience)
  2. The customer is the hero, not you — (Miller/StoryBrand)
  3. Reciprocity drives everything — Give value first, the sale follows (Cialdini)
  4. Specificity beats cleverness — "At 60mph, the loudest noise..." outsells "luxury ride" (Ogilvy)
  5. Social proof is the shortcut — People do what other people do (Cialdini)
  6. Repetition builds trust — Don't change your message because you're bored (Levinson)
  7. Scarcity creates urgency — Honest scarcity moves people. Fake scarcity destroys you (Cialdini)
  8. Remarkable beats good — "Very good" is invisible (Godin)
  9. Clarity beats complexity — If you confuse, you lose (Miller)
  10. Follow-up is where the money lives — 68% of lost business is poor follow-up (Levinson)
  11. Price signals value — Cheap attracts bad clients and thin margins (Hormozi, Godin)
  12. Sell the transformation, not the product — Nobody wants a drill. They want a beautiful home.
