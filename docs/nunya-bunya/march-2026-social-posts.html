If your law firm isn't showing up on page one of Google, you're basically invisible to 90% of the people searching for a lawyer right now.

Harsh? Sure. But it's also just math.

Post 8: Facebook

Publish date: Wednesday, March 18

Post type: Single image (quote graphic: "A 1-star review doesn't define your firm. How you respond to it does.")

Copy:

Let's talk about bad reviews. Because every law firm gets them eventually.

A disgruntled opposing party. A client who didn't understand the legal process. Someone who's just having a bad day. It happens.

Here's what matters: how you respond.

A thoughtful, professional response to a negative review does more for your reputation than the review itself ever could. Potential clients reading that response are thinking: "Okay, this firm handles pressure with grace. That's who I want in my corner."

Three rules for responding to bad reviews:

  • Never get defensive
  • Acknowledge the concern without admitting fault
  • Take the conversation offline

And for the love of everything — never, ever ignore them.

Week 4 — Content Marketing Strategy

Post 10: LinkedIn

Publish date: Monday, March 23

Post type: Carousel (5 slides — content framework visual)

Copy:

Most law firms treat content like homework. Something they know they "should" do, so they crank out a 400-word blog once a month about "5 things to do after a car accident" and call it strategy.

That's not content marketing. That's content existing.

Real content marketing for law firms starts with one question: what does your ideal client need to know before they're ready to hire you?

Then you build a library of answers — blogs, videos, FAQs, case studies — that meets them at every stage. The person Googling "do I have a case" isn't ready for a consult yet. But the firm that answers that question earns the right to be top of mind when they are.

The firms that commit to this for 6–12 months build a compounding asset. Every piece of content keeps working while you sleep, attracting and nurturing the exact people you want to sign.

What's your firm's biggest content challenge — finding time, finding topics, or not knowing where to start?
