Before the Camera

A Portrait Posing Guide for Creative Professionals

How to show up with intention — whether it's your first portrait session or your twentieth.

By Power Portraits Photography

Introduction

A good photographer will direct you. That much is true, and that much you can count on. But the best sessions — the ones where something extraordinary happens between lens and subject — those begin before the camera ever comes out.

This guide is for the actor updating headshots between seasons. The musician who needs press photos that feel as alive as the music. The solopreneur whose website still has that cropped photo from a friend's wedding. It is for anyone who understands that a portrait is not decoration — it is communication.

What follows is everything we've learned about how people show up well in front of a camera. Not poses to memorise. Not tricks to perform. Just a way of arriving that makes the rest feel effortless.

The Foundation — Posture & Presence

Standing

The difference between a portrait that commands attention and one that merely occupies space often comes down to how weight is distributed across two feet.

Weight: Shift roughly 60% of your weight onto your back foot. This creates a natural, slight angle in the body — far more flattering than standing square to the camera like you're waiting for a bus.

Shoulders: Roll them back and down. Not military-stiff — just settled. Think of it as the posture you'd adopt if someone you admired walked into the room.

Spine: Lengthen through the crown of your head. Imagine a thread pulling you gently upward. This alone transforms how the camera reads you.

Sitting

Perch on the front edge of the seat. Never sink back. The moment you lean into a chair, your posture collapses and your energy retreats. Sitting forward communicates engagement — and engagement photographs as confidence.

Keep your hands in your lap, on your knees, or resting lightly on a surface beside you. Avoid gripping armrests.

The Reset

Between setups — between outfits, between locations, between one series of frames and the next — shake it off. Drop your shoulders. Roll your neck. Take a breath that fills your lungs completely and let it out slowly. This is not indulgence. It is technique. Accumulated tension shows in every frame, and the reset is how you clear it.

Breathing

When the camera is pointed at you, your instinct will be to hold your breath. Resist this. Breathe normally. Better yet, breathe slowly and deliberately. A held breath creates tension in the jaw, the shoulders, the hands — places the camera reads with unforgiving clarity.

Key principle: Tension shows. Comfort shows. Choose comfort.

The Face — Expression & Angles

The Jaw

This is the single most impactful adjustment most people can make. Push your jaw slightly forward and down — as if you're reaching toward the camera with your chin. It feels unnatural. It looks extraordinary. It eliminates the soft line beneath the chin and creates the kind of definition you see in magazine editorials.

Practice this in a mirror until it becomes muscle memory.

The Eyes

A slight squint — what photographers call a "squinch" — transforms a portrait. This is not a full squint. It is a narrowing of the lower eyelid only, by perhaps 10-15%. The result is an expression that reads as confident, engaged, and present. Wide-open eyes tend to read as startled.

Think of the expression you'd make if someone across the room said something quietly brilliant.

The Mouth

Three options, each with a purpose:

  • Slightly parted lips — relaxed, natural, editorial. This is the default for most portrait work.
  • Closed mouth, soft expression — composed, serious, authoritative. Works beautifully for corporate and professional use.
  • Smile — genuine warmth. But the smile must reach the eyes (a "Duchenne smile"), or it reads as performance. Think of something that actually amuses you.

Turning the Face

  • Three-quarter view (turning your face roughly 30-45 degrees from the camera) — the most universally flattering angle. It creates depth and dimension.
  • Straight-on — direct, confrontational, powerful. Use it when you want to command.
  • Profile — dramatic, editorial, distinctive. Not for every purpose, but striking when it fits.
Key principle: The camera sees stillness as confidence. Small movements, not big ones.

Hands & Body Language

Hands are where most people's discomfort becomes visible. The solution is not to hide them — it is to give them purpose.

What Works

  • In pockets — one hand, not both. Thumb out or fingers out, either works. Both hands in pockets reads as defensive.
  • On hips — creates angles and structure. One hand, lightly resting. Avoid the "power stance" with both hands — it reads as confrontational in a still image.
  • Crossed arms — can work if the expression is warm. If the expression is also closed, the combination reads as hostile.
  • Holding something — a jacket over the shoulder, a guitar, a book, a coffee cup. Props give hands narrative.

The Actor's Trick

Give your hands an action. Adjusting a cuff. Touching a collar. Resting on a surface. The hand doesn't need to be doing something dramatic — it just needs to be doing something. Idle hands fidget, and fidgeting reads as uncertainty.

What to Avoid

  • Clenched fists (reads as aggression or anxiety)
  • Stiff, straight fingers (reads as robotic)
  • Hands flat on surfaces (reads as awkward)
  • Fingers spread wide (reads as tense)
Key principle: If your hands look uncomfortable, the whole image looks uncomfortable.

Wardrobe Quick Reference

What you wear shapes the photograph as much as how you stand in it. A few principles:

Colour: Solid colours photograph best. Deep, rich tones — navy, charcoal, burgundy, black, cream, forest green — create a grounded, sophisticated palette. They recede, letting your face remain the focal point.

Texture: Linen, leather, denim, knits, velvet — these fabrics catch light beautifully and add dimension. A textured blazer photographs more interestingly than a smooth polyester one, even if the polyester costs more.

Avoid: Neon colours (they cast unflattering reflections onto skin). Busy patterns (they compete with your face for attention). Large logos (they date the image and distract). Anything you haven't worn before (discomfort with clothing translates directly to discomfort in the image).

Iron everything. Wrinkles that are invisible in person become the loudest element in a photograph. Steam or press every piece you plan to wear.

Bring options. More than you think you'll need. Three to five outfits for a standard session. We'll choose together on the day based on the locations and the light.

Accessories tell stories. Glasses, jewellery, hats, scarves, watches, instruments — these details are not distractions. They are character.

The Mindset

Almost everyone walks into a portrait session with some version of the same thought: I don't know what I'm doing in front of a camera.

Good. You're not supposed to. That's our job.

Your job is simply to show up — rested, hydrated, wearing something you feel good in — and trust the process. We will direct every angle, every expression, every shift of weight. You don't need to bring poses you've rehearsed in a mirror. You need to bring yourself.

Nerves are normal. They pass. Usually within the first ten minutes. The first few frames exist partly to let you settle in. The portraits that matter come after the self-consciousness dissolves.

Music helps. If you have a playlist that puts you in the right headspace — confident, relaxed, energised — bring it. We'll play it during the session. It changes the atmosphere more than you'd expect.

You don't need to perform. The best portraits are not performances. They are moments of stillness where something true becomes visible. Show up as yourself, slightly turned up.

The best portrait we've ever taken of someone was not the first frame. It was the one after they forgot the camera was there.
