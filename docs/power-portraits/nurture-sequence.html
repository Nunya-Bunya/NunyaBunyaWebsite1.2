Email 2 of 5 — Day 3

Purpose: Remove uncertainty about the session experience. Answer the unasked questions that prevent booking.

Subject: What actually happens at a portrait session

Alt Subject: Behind the scenes at Power Portraits

Preview Text: From the moment you walk in to the moment your gallery arrives — here's how it works.

Mautic Tags: nurture-session-walkthrough

Mautic Notes: High-intent signal if contact clicks the booking link in this email. Apply tag nurture-clicked-book for segmentation.

<p>Hi {{contact.firstname|default('there')}},</p>

<p>Most people who think about booking a portrait session don't hesitate because of the price. They hesitate because they don't know what to expect — and uncertainty is the enemy of action.</p>

<p>So here's exactly what a session at Power Portraits looks like, start to finish.</p>

<h3 style="color: #1a1a1a; margin-top: 25px;">Before the session</h3>

<p>You'll receive a pre-session questionnaire covering your goals, wardrobe preferences, and where you plan to use the images. We also send our Session Prep Guide — practical advice on clothing, grooming, and how to arrive feeling camera-ready. If you booked The Portrait or The Editorial, we'll schedule a style consultation or brand strategy call beforehand so every frame has purpose.</p>

<h3 style="color: #1a1a1a; margin-top: 25px;">On the day</h3>

<p>Sessions run between one and three hours depending on your package. The studio atmosphere is calm — good light, good music, no rush. You'll receive professional direction throughout. We don't hand you a prop and hope for the best; we guide your posture, expression, and energy in real time. Multiple locations and wardrobe changes are built into most packages, so you leave with genuine variety — not thirty versions of the same shot.</p>

<h3 style="color: #1a1a1a; margin-top: 25px;">What to wear</h3>

<p>Solid colours photograph best. Avoid busy patterns, large logos, and anything you haven't worn recently (you want to feel like yourself, not a costume). Bring two to three outfit options. Layers are your friend — a jacket over a shirt gives you two looks in thirty seconds.</p>

<h3 style="color: #1a1a1a; margin-top: 25px;">After the session</h3>

<p>Within 5–7 business days (3–5 for Editorial clients), you'll receive a private online gallery. Choose your favourites, we professionally retouch them — colour grading, skin refinement, the kind of careful work that honours the person in the frame without making them look like someone else — and deliver high-resolution files ready for print, web, and social media. Every image comes in multiple aspect ratios: 1:1, 4:5, 2:3, and 16:9.</p>

<p>That's it. No hidden steps, no surprise upsells, no awkward sales pitch at the end. Just considered portraiture from start to finish.</p>

Email 3 of 5 — Day 7

Purpose: Make the case for professional portraiture as an investment, not an expense. Use real, publicly verifiable data.

Subject: The headshot that pays for itself

Alt Subject: Why your profile photo matters more than your resume

Preview Text: The data on first impressions is hard to argue with.

Mautic Tags: nurture-roi-angle

Mautic Notes: This email performs the "why now" function. If contact opens but doesn't click, consider a follow-up nudge at Day 10 (optional, not included in this sequence).

<p>Hi {{contact.firstname|default('there')}},</p>

<p>There's a piece of research that changed the way we think about what we do.</p>

<p>Princeton psychologists Janine Willis and Alexander Todorov found that people form judgements about competence, trustworthiness, and likability within <strong>one-tenth of a second</strong> of seeing a face. Not a conversation. Not a handshake. A photograph.</p>

<p>That finding has been replicated across dozens of studies since 2006. And it has practical consequences that are hard to ignore:</p>

<ul>
  <li>LinkedIn profiles with professional headshots receive <strong>14 times more profile views</strong> than those without (LinkedIn's own published data).</li>
  <li>Recruiters spend an average of <strong>19% of their time on a profile looking at the photo</strong> — more than on the summary, skills, or experience sections (TheLadders eye-tracking study).</li>
  <li>Profiles with photos are <strong>36 times more likely</strong> to receive a message on LinkedIn (LinkedIn data, widely cited in their business resources).</li>
</ul>

<p>None of this is surprising when you think about it. We are visual creatures operating in an increasingly visual economy. Your headshot is the first thing people see on LinkedIn, your company website, your email signature, your speaker bio, your press kit. It shapes the story before you've said a word.</p>

<p>And yet — most professionals are still using a photo taken by a friend at a wedding, or a cropped group shot from a work event, or a selfie taken in their car. Not because they don't care. Because updating it never felt urgent enough.</p>

<p>We'd argue it's one of the highest-leverage investments a professional can make. Not because we sell photography — but because the evidence is genuinely compelling. A portrait that communicates confidence, approachability, and competence works for you around the clock, across every platform, in every introduction you'll never know happened.</p>

<p>When you're ready to make the upgrade, we're here.</p>

Key Principles

  • No fabricated testimonials. Every quote used must be from a real client with their permission. Placeholder brackets are used where social proof would go — fill these only with verified quotes.
  • No numeric guarantees. We cite real, publicly available research but never promise specific outcomes ("book more clients", "get X more views").
  • No fake urgency. No countdown timers, no "only 3 spots left", no manufactured scarcity. The brand is premium and unhurried.
  • Real data only. The LinkedIn statistics cited in Email 3 are from publicly available LinkedIn and TheLadders research. Verify current accuracy before launch.
  • Posts as the brand. Sign-off is "Power Portraits", never Ben personally (unlike the strategy doc's literary sequence which was written in Ben's voice — this nurture sequence uses the brand voice).
