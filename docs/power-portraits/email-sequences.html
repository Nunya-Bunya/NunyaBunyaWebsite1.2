Email 2 of 4 — Day 2: The Story

Trigger: 2 days after Email 1

Subject: Why we photograph creatives.

[FIRST NAME],

I want to tell you something about why this studio exists.

I didn't set out to photograph creatives. I set out to photograph people who were doing something interesting with their lives — and creatives kept walking through the door. Actors between auditions, musicians about to release something they'd spent years on, business owners who'd finally outgrown the headshot their mate took in a car park.

What they had in common wasn't vanity. It was the opposite. They'd been so busy making things that they'd neglected the most basic piece of their own story: an image that actually looked like them. Not the version of them from three years ago, or the version their phone camera approximated in bad light, but them — present tense, in full command of who they are.

That's the work I fell in love with. Not making people look good (though we do). Making people look recognisable. Giving a creative person an image that matches the quality of what they create.

I started Power Portraits because I believe the people who make culture deserve better than what most photography studios offer — rushed sessions, generic backdrops, the same retouching applied to every face. Creatives deserve considered, unhurried, genuinely skilled portraiture.

That's what we do here.

Nothing to sell you today. I just wanted you to know who's on the other end of these emails.

Speak soon,

Email 4 of 4 — Day 10: The Perspective Piece

Trigger: 5 days after Email 3

Subject: The portrait you haven't taken yet.

[FIRST NAME],

There is a version of you that exists right now — this week, this month — that will never exist again.

Not in a melancholy way. In the way a musician understands that the songs they write at twenty-four come from a different person than the songs they'll write at forty. In the way an actor knows that the roles they're right for today are shaped by a face and a presence that is, quietly and without permission, changing.

Portraiture has always been about this. Not preservation — that's what snapshots do. Portraiture is about declaration. It says: this is who I am, and I know it, and I'm not apologising for any of it.

The great portraits in history weren't taken because someone needed a new headshot. They were taken because someone understood that the way they looked right now was worth recording with intention. With skill. With the kind of attention that most photographs never receive.

I think about this often in the studio. A person sits in front of me, and I'm not just thinking about light and angle. I'm thinking about the fact that they showed up. That they decided their own image was worth an hour of considered work. There's something quietly radical about that, especially for creatives — people who spend most of their energy making things for others.

The portrait you haven't taken yet is the one that captures you as you are right now. Not the version of you that existed when your current headshot was taken. Not the future version you're working towards. The one sitting here reading this email, in the middle of whatever you're in the middle of.

That version of you is worth photographing.

Ben Alek Conner
