Executive Summary

Nunya Bunya is a Brisbane-based AI-powered marketing agency that delivers comprehensive digital marketing solutions to small businesses across the region. The company operates as a full-service marketing machine, handling website development, advertising campaigns, email marketing, content creation, and analytics through the strategic implementation of artificial intelligence and open-source technologies. This approach enables the solo founder to scale operations efficiently while maintaining high-quality service delivery. Currently in the pre-revenue phase with fully developed infrastructure, Nunya Bunya has successfully served internal clients and is actively pursuing its first external paying customers. The business operates with flexible Monday-Saturday availability, delivering services entirely through remote/online channels with client meetings conducted via video calls or in-person sessions within Brisbane CBD. The company's unique positioning combines the personalised attention of a boutique agency with the scalability and efficiency of AI-driven automation, targeting the underserved Brisbane small business market.

Market Analysis

The AI-powered digital marketing industry is experiencing explosive growth, with the global AI in marketing market valued at $15.8 billion in 2021 and projected to reach $107.5 billion by 2028. This growth is driven by increasing demand for personalisation, automation of repetitive tasks, and the need for data-driven decision making. Small and medium businesses are particularly attracted to AI marketing solutions as they offer enterprise-level capabilities at accessible price points, enabling them to compete with larger competitors who have traditionally dominated digital marketing. However, the industry faces significant challenges including client education about AI capabilities versus limitations, maintaining the human touch in an increasingly automated landscape, and navigating the complexity of integrating multiple AI tools effectively. Many agencies struggle with over-promising AI capabilities or failing to deliver measurable ROI, leading to client scepticism. The most successful agencies focus on transparency, education, and demonstrating clear value through improved efficiency and results rather than relying on AI as a marketing buzzword.

Local Market

Brisbane's small business market represents a significant opportunity, with over 87,000 small businesses operating across Southeast Queensland generating combined revenue exceeding $45 billion annually. The city's strong economy, driven by construction, professional services, and a growing tech sector, has created a robust ecosystem of businesses that understand the need for digital marketing but often lack the resources or expertise to execute effectively. Brisbane's relatively conservative business culture means many small business owners are still catching up on digital transformation, creating substantial demand for accessible, results-focused marketing solutions. The local competitive landscape is fragmented, with traditional agencies like Clearwater and Digital Eagles commanding premium prices that put them out of reach for many small businesses, while the freelancer market is oversaturated but inconsistent in quality and reliability. This creates a clear gap for a technology-enabled agency that can deliver enterprise-quality services at small business prices. Brisbane's geographic spread across multiple suburbs also favours agencies that can deliver high-quality services remotely, rather than requiring frequent in-person meetings that add time and cost.

Legal & Compliance

Business Registration

Australian Business Number (ABN) registration mandatory for tax purposes. GST registration required once annual turnover exceeds $75,000. Business name registration through ASIC if operating under 'Nunya Bunya' rather than personal name. Queensland state business registration may be required depending on specific activities.

Licenses & Permits

No specific industry licences required for digital marketing services in Australia. However, if providing financial advice about marketing ROI or business strategy, consider whether an Australian Financial Services (AFS) licence is needed. Privacy legislation compliance is mandatory when handling client data.

Insurance

Professional Indemnity insurance essential (minimum $1-5M coverage) for advice and services provided. Public Liability insurance recommended ($5-10M coverage) for any in-person meetings or Brisbane CBD activities. Cyber Liability insurance strongly recommended given data handling and AI processing of client information. Product Liability may be needed if productizing services.

Industry Bodies

Australian Marketing Institute (AMI) membership provides credibility and professional development. Australian Direct Marketing Association (ADMA) relevant for email marketing compliance. Consider membership in Australian Computer Society (ACS) for technology aspects and Artificial Intelligence Institute of Australia for emerging AI regulations.

Privacy

Australian Privacy Principles (APPs) under Privacy Act 1988 apply to handling client business data. Must have privacy policy covering data collection, use, storage, and disclosure practices. GDPR compliance may be required if serving international clients. Notifiable data breach scheme requires reporting breaches to OAIC within 72 hours.

Consumer Law

Australian Consumer Law guarantees apply to all services - must provide services with due care and skill, fit for purpose, and within reasonable timeframe. Clear terms and conditions required covering service scope, refund policies, and dispute resolution. Misleading and deceptive conduct provisions apply to all marketing claims about service outcomes.

WH&S

As a sole trader working from home office, basic workplace health and safety applies - ergonomic workstation setup, electrical safety, mental health considerations. If hiring staff, full WHS obligations under Work Health and Safety Act 2011 apply including risk assessments and safety procedures.

Red Flags

  • Making guarantees about marketing results or ROI that could be seen as misleading conduct
  • Using client data for AI training without explicit consent
  • Storing client data on overseas servers without proper privacy safeguards
  • Claiming qualifications, certifications, or industry memberships that don't exist
  • Providing financial or business advice that could require AFS licensing
  • Using copyrighted content in AI-generated materials without permission
  • Not having proper contracts with clear scope limitations and liability clauses
  • Failing to register for GST once turnover threshold is reached
  • Not maintaining adequate insurance coverage for professional services
  • Violating platform terms of service for LinkedIn, Google, or other tools used

Our Origin Story

The cursor blinked on line 847 of code at 2:17 AM on a Tuesday in Brisbane. Another sleepless night, another system to build. I'd started as a filmmaker and photographer, but somewhere between trying to market my own work and teaching myself to code, I'd accidentally become obsessed with automation. Seven businesses later, I had 43 AI agents running automated marketing pipelines on a server that cost less than a decent lunch. The infrastructure was beautiful, efficient, almost artistic in its complexity. But it was also completely pointless — I'd built the perfect marketing machine with no one to market to. Then came the test. A massage therapist in Brisbane needed a complete brand strategy. Ten minutes later, I was staring at 176KB of genuinely premium work: brand identity, marketing playbooks, ad copy, email sequences, 90-day implementation plan. It was better than what most agencies delivered in six weeks, and it had cost practically nothing to generate. That's when it hit me: while I'd been building systems for myself, small businesses everywhere were getting ripped off by agencies charging $5K monthly for mediocre work. The solution already existed in my server. I just needed to turn it into a business.

Logo Usage Rules

Do

  • Maintain clear space around the logo
  • Use on white or dark backgrounds
  • Keep proportions — never stretch
  • Use provided variations for dark backgrounds
  • Use high-resolution files for print

Don’t

  • Don’t place on busy backgrounds
  • Don’t change the logo colours
  • Don’t add effects (shadows, gradients)
  • Don’t rotate or skew the logo
  • Don’t use low-res versions for print

Photography Style

Aim For

  • Behind-the-scenes shots of actual work - code on screens, strategy sessions, late-night problem solving
  • Natural lighting whenever possible - window light beats harsh office fluorescents
  • Authentic moments of concentration, collaboration, or breakthrough 'aha' reactions
  • Tools of the trade in action - laptops, notebooks, coffee cups, sketches on napkins
  • Diverse perspectives and working styles - remote work, different environments, various ages and backgrounds
  • Results that matter - happy client reactions, positive metrics on screens, real impact

Avoid

  • Stock photo handshakes, forced smiles, or people pointing at charts
  • Overly staged 'brainstorming' with actors writing buzzwords on whiteboards
  • Generic office environments or sterile conference rooms
  • Anyone wearing a suit unless they genuinely work in suits
  • Fake diversity or obviously posed 'collaboration'
